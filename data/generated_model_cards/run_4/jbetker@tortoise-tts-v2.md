## Model Details
This section provides fundamental information about the model, helping stakeholders understand its context and key characteristics.

### Person or organization developing model:
The model was developed by James Betker (setup.py; 2305.07243.pdf, p. 1). The author is described as an independent researcher (2305.07243.pdf, p. 12).

### Model date:
The models were trained over a period of 1 year (2305.07243.pdf, p. 4). The associated research paper was submitted to arXiv on May 23, 2023 (2305.07243.pdf, p. 1). The version specified in the setup file is 2.1.3 (setup.py).

### Model version:
The model is named TorToise-v2 (2305.07243.pdf, p. 3). The package version is 2.1.3 (setup.py). This system is a multi-component text-to-speech framework that differs from traditional encoder-decoder models by combining a GPT-2-style autoregressive model with a diffusion model (2305.07243.pdf, p. 1, 3).

### Model type:
TorToise is an expressive, multi-voice text-to-speech (TTS) system (2305.07243.pdf, p. 1). It is composed of several neural networks that work in sequence to convert text and a reference audio clip into a high-quality speech output (2305.07243.pdf, p. 3).

The main components are:
*   **Autoregressive (AR) Model (`UnifiedVoice`):** This is a GPT-2 based transformer decoder that predicts a sequence of speech tokens conditioned on text tokens and a speech conditioning input (tortoise/models/autoregressive.py; 2305.07243.pdf, p. 3, 8).
    *   **Architecture:** Transformer stack with 30 layers, a model dimension of 1024, and 16 attention heads (tortoise/models/autoregressive.py; 2305.07243.pdf, p. 9).
    *   **Context Length:** Supports a maximum of 402 text tokens and 604 MEL tokens (tortoise/models/autoregressive.py; 2305.07243.pdf, p. 8).

*   **Diffusion Decoder (`DiffusionTts`):** This model converts the speech tokens (or latent vectors from the AR model) into a MEL spectrogram (tortoise/models/diffusion_decoder.py; 2305.07243.pdf, p. 3).
    *   **Architecture:** A bespoke architecture combining residual convolutions with 10 layers of dense self-attention, resembling a U-Net but without upsampling or downsampling (tortoise/models/diffusion_decoder.py; 2305.07243.pdf, p. 11). It has a model dimension of 1024 and 16 attention heads (tortoise/models/diffusion_decoder.py; 2305.07243.pdf, p. 12).

*   **Contrastive Language-Voice Pretrained Transformer (CLVP):** A model similar to CLIP, trained to produce distance metrics for text/speech pairs. It is used to rank the multiple outputs from the AR model to select the best candidate (tortoise/models/clvp.py; 2305.07243.pdf, p. 4).
    *   **Architecture:** Dual transformer stacks with a depth of 12 (20 in the paper), model dimension of 512 (768 in the paper), and 8 attention heads (12 in the paper) (tortoise/models/clvp.py; 2305.07243.pdf, p. 10).

*   **Contrastive Voice-Voice Pretrained Transformer (CVVP):** Another contrastive model used alongside CLVP to filter the AR model's outputs, focusing on matching the voice characteristics of the reference clip (tortoise/api.py; tortoise/models/cvvp.py).

*   **Vocoder (`UnivNetGenerator`):** A pre-trained UnivNet model is used to convert the final MEL spectrogram into a raw audio waveform (tortoise/models/vocoder.py; 2305.07243.pdf, p. 1).

*   **Classifier (`AudioMiniEncoderWithClassifierHead`):** A small audio encoder with a classifier head trained to determine if an audio clip was generated by Tortoise (tortoise/is_this_from_tortoise.py; tortoise/models/classifier.py).

### Training details:
The models were trained on a small cluster of 8 NVIDIA RTX-3090s over a period of 1 year (2305.07243.pdf, p. 4).

*   **Autoregressive Model:**
    *   **Architecture:** A standard GPT-2 architecture with 30 layers, 1024 model dimensions, and 16 heads (2305.07243.pdf, p. 9).
    *   **Training:** Trained for 119M samples with a batch size of 1024. The loss was a weighted sum of text and MEL token prediction (cross-entropy), with weights of 0.01 and 1 respectively. It used an Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 1e-4, B1=0.9, B2=0.96, and weight decay of 0.01. A 500-step learning rate warmup was used (2305.07243.pdf, p. 9).

*   **Diffusion Decoder:**
    *   **Training:** The model was first trained to convert discrete speech codes into MEL spectrograms. After convergence, it was fine-tuned on the latent space from the AR model's outputs, a process termed the "Tortoise Trick" to improve efficiency and quality (2305.07243.pdf, p. 4).
    *   **Parameters:** Trained for 65M samples with a batch size of 512. The loss function was MSE + VLB. It used an Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 1e-5, B1=0.9, B2=0.999, and weight decay of 0.001. A 1000-step learning rate warmup was used (2305.07243.pdf, p. 12).
    *   **Guidance:** The model uses classifier-free guidance on both the speech conditioning signal and the AR model activations. During training, these signals were dropped 15% of the time and replaced with a learned embedding (2305.07243.pdf, p. 11).

*   **CLVP Model:**
    *   **Training:** Trained on 80M samples of text/speech pairs with a batch size of 1024 using a contrastive loss (2305.07243.pdf, p. 10).
    *   **Parameters:** Used an Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 3e-4, B1=0.9, B2=0.96, and weight decay of 0.001. A 500-step learning rate warmup was used (2305.07243.pdf, p. 10).

*   **Inference Sampling:**
    *   **Autoregressive:** Nucleus sampling (top-p) is used with P=0.8, a repetition penalty of 2.0, and a softmax temperature of 0.8 (2305.07243.pdf, p. 4).
    *   **Diffusion:** DDIM sampling is used with a linear schedule, 64 sampling steps, and a conditioning-free guidance constant of 2 (2305.07243.pdf, p. 5).

## Intended Use
This section outlines the intended applications of the model.

### Primary intended uses:
The primary intended use of TorToise is for high-quality, multi-voice text-to-speech (TTS) synthesis (setup.py; 2305.07243.pdf, p. 1). Its key capabilities include:
*   **Voice Cloning:** Generating speech in a specific voice using one or more ~10-second reference audio clips (tortoise/api.py).
*   **Voice Combination:** Creating a new voice with traits from multiple parent voices by combining their reference clips (tortoise-tts.ipynb).
*   **Random Voice Generation:** The model can generate speech in a random voice without any reference audio (tortoise-tts.ipynb).
*   **Long-form Reading:** The model can read long text files by splitting them into smaller segments and synthesizing each part (tortoise/read.py).

The model takes text and one or more voice samples as input and produces a 24kHz audio waveform as output (tortoise/api.py).

### Primary intended users:
The intended users include researchers, developers, and anyone interested in high-quality text-to-speech synthesis (inferred from the open-source nature and provided scripts like `do_tts.py` and `read.py`).

### Out-of-scope uses:
*   **Low-latency applications:** The model is named "Tortoise" to signify its slow inference speed, which can take up to a minute for a single sentence on a GPU. It is not suitable for real-time or low-latency use cases (tortoise-tts.ipynb).
*   **Misuse for Impersonation:** The voice cloning capability could be misused to create synthetic audio for malicious purposes, such as impersonation or creating deepfakes. The repository provides a classifier to detect audio generated by Tortoise, which can be a mitigation against such misuse (tortoise/is_this_from_tortoise.py).

## How to Use
This section outlines how to use the model.

The model can be used via Python scripts or a command-line interface. A GPU is strongly recommended for inference (tortoise-tts.ipynb).

**Python API Usage:**
First, install the necessary dependencies and the package itself:
```bash
!git clone https://github.com/neonbjb/tortoise-tts.git
%cd tortoise-tts
!pip3 install -r requirements.txt
!python3 setup.py install
```
(from tortoise-tts.ipynb)

Then, you can use the `TextToSpeech` class to generate audio:
```python
import torch
import torchaudio
import IPython
from tortoise.api import TextToSpeech
from tortoise.utils.audio import load_voice, load_voices

# This will download all the models used by Tortoise from the HF hub.
tts = TextToSpeech()

# The text to be spoken.
text = "The expressiveness of autoregressive transformers is literally nuts! I absolutely adore them."

# Pick a preset mode for quality. Options: {"ultra_fast", "fast", "standard", "high_quality"}.
preset = "fast"

# Generate with a pre-packaged voice
voice = 'tom'
voice_samples, conditioning_latents = load_voice(voice)
gen = tts.tts_with_preset(text, voice_samples=voice_samples, conditioning_latents=conditioning_latents, preset=preset)
torchaudio.save('generated_tom.wav', gen.squeeze(0).cpu(), 24000)
IPython.display.Audio('generated_tom.wav')

# Generate with a random voice
gen_random = tts.tts_with_preset(text, voice_samples=None, conditioning_latents=None, preset=preset)
torchaudio.save('generated_random.wav', gen_random.squeeze(0).cpu(), 24000)
IPython.display.Audio('generated_random.wav')

# Combine voices
voice_samples_combined, conditioning_latents_combined = load_voices(['pat', 'william'])
gen_combined = tts.tts_with_preset("They used to say that if man was meant to fly, he’d have wings. But he did fly. He discovered he had to.", 
                                   voice_samples=voice_samples_combined, conditioning_latents=conditioning_latents_combined, preset=preset)
torchaudio.save('captain_kirkard.wav', gen_combined.squeeze(0).cpu(), 24000)
IPython.display.Audio('captain_kirkard.wav')
```
(from tortoise-tts.ipynb; tortoise/do_tts.py)

## Factors
This section addresses variables that may impact the model's performance.

### Relevant factors:
*   **Voice Samples:** The quality, tone, and prosody of the generated speech are inferred from the provided reference audio clips (the "speech conditioning input") (2305.07243.pdf, p. 4). The quality and characteristics of these samples are a key factor.
*   **`clvp_cvvp_slider`:** This parameter controls the balance between adhering to the text (intelligibility) and mimicking the reference voice's characteristics. A value closer to 1 prioritizes text-following, while a value closer to 0 prioritizes voice similarity (tortoise/api.py).
*   **Generation Parameters:** Various parameters in the `tts` method, such as `temperature`, `repetition_penalty`, and `diffusion_iterations`, influence the diversity and quality of the output (tortoise/api.py).

### Evaluation factors:
The author built an evaluation suite that uses:
*   **CLVP distance metric:** To measure the similarity between real and generated samples, analogous to the FID score in image generation (2305.07243.pdf, p. 5).
*   **Wav2vec model:** To characterize the "intelligibility" of a speech segment (2305.07243.pdf, p. 5).

## Metrics
This section describes how the model's performance is evaluated.

### Model performance measures:
The paper does not report standard TTS metrics like Mean Opinion Score (MOS). Instead, it mentions a custom evaluation suite that uses:
*   A **CLVP-based distance metric** to compare real and generated samples (2305.07243.pdf, p. 5).
*   An open-source **wav2vec model** to measure the "intelligibility" of the speech (2305.07243.pdf, p. 5).

### Decision thresholds:
During inference, the model generates a large number of candidate speech outputs (`num_autoregressive_samples`). The CLVP and CVVP models are then used to score the correlation between each candidate and the input text/voice. The top `k` candidates with the highest scores are selected for the final diffusion and vocoding steps (tortoise/api.py; 2305.07243.pdf, p. 4). The `clvp_cvvp_slider` parameter acts as a threshold to balance the influence of text similarity (CLVP) and voice similarity (CVVP) in this ranking process (tortoise/api.py).

### Variation approaches:
The model uses a re-ranking process. It first generates many (`num_autoregressive_samples`) speech candidates from the autoregressive model. These candidates are then scored and ranked using the CLVP and CVVP models to select the best one(s) for the final, computationally expensive diffusion step. This allows for exploring the output distribution of the probabilistic AR model and selecting for high-quality samples (tortoise/api.py; 2305.07243.pdf, p. 2, 4).

## Training Data
This section provides details about the datasets used to train the model.

### Datasets:
The model was trained on a combination of datasets:
1.  **LibriTTS:** A corpus derived from LibriSpeech for text-to-speech (Zen et al., 2019, cited in 2305.07243.pdf, p. 6).
2.  **HiFiTTS:** A multi-speaker English TTS dataset (Bakhturina et al., 2021, cited in 2305.07243.pdf, p. 5).
    *   These two datasets combined contain 896 hours of transcribed speech (2305.07243.pdf, p. 5).
3.  **Extended Dataset:** An independently built dataset of 49,000 hours of cleaned audio clips from audiobooks and podcasts scraped from the web (2305.07243.pdf, p. 5, 6).

### Motivation:
The goal was to train a large language model for speech, which required a very large amount of data (2305.07243.pdf, p. 5).

### Preprocessing:
The extended dataset underwent a significant preprocessing pipeline:
*   Audio was split on 500ms silences, and clips between 5-20 seconds were kept.
*   A pipeline of classifiers was used to filter the clips, removing any audio with background noise, music, poor quality (e.g., phone calls), multiple speakers, or reverb.
*   The cleaned audio was transcribed using a wav2vec2-large model that was fine-tuned to predict punctuation (commas, quotation marks, etc.) (2305.07243.pdf, p. 6).

## Memory or Hardware Requirements
This section outlines the memory or hardware requirements for loading, deploying, and training the model.

### Loading Requirements:
A GPU is strongly recommended for inference. The `autoregressive_batch_size` parameter in the `TextToSpeech` class can be lowered to avoid GPU Out-Of-Memory (OOM) errors, suggesting significant VRAM usage (tortoise/api.py; tortoise-tts.ipynb).

### Deploying Requirements:
Inference is slow. A single sentence can take up to a minute on a GPU and hours on a CPU (tortoise-tts.ipynb). This makes the model unsuitable for real-time deployment.

### Training or Fine-tuning Requirements:
The models were trained on a small cluster of 8 NVIDIA RTX-3090s over a period of 1 year (2305.07243.pdf, p. 4).

## Ethical Considerations
This section discusses the ethical considerations in model development, including challenges, risks, and solutions.

*   **Misuse Potential:** The model's ability to clone voices from short audio clips presents a risk of misuse for creating deepfakes, impersonation, and spreading misinformation.
*   **Mitigation:** The repository includes a script, `is_this_from_tortoise.py`, which uses a trained classifier to detect whether an audio clip was generated by Tortoise. This can serve as a tool to identify synthetic media created by this model (tortoise/is_this_from_tortoise.py; tortoise/api.py).
*   **Data Sourcing:** The 49,000-hour extended training dataset was created by scraping audiobooks and podcasts from the web (2305.07243.pdf, p. 6). This may raise copyright concerns, although the paper does not discuss this.
*   **Content Generation:** The model is capable of generating profane language, as evidenced by the inclusion of `cursed_copypasta.txt` in the repository, which contains explicit content. This indicates a lack of explicit content filtering at the generation stage.
*   **Redaction Feature:** The API includes an `enable_redaction` option, which automatically removes text enclosed in `[]` from the spoken output by aligning the text to the audio and cutting the corresponding segments. This could potentially be used to control the output and prevent the model from speaking certain words or phrases (tortoise/api.py; tortoise/utils/wav2vec_alignment.py).

## Caveats and Recommendations
This section lists unresolved issues and provides guidance for users.

### Caveats:
*   **Inference Speed:** The model is very slow, as its name "Tortoise" implies. It is not suitable for applications requiring low latency (tortoise-tts.ipynb).
*   **Sequence Length Limitation:** The autoregressive model uses fixed positional encodings, which limits the total length of speech it can produce in a single pass. The maximum sequence length is 402 text tokens and 604 MEL tokens (2305.07243.pdf, p. 8, 12). For longer texts, manual splitting is required (tortoise/read.py).
*   **Re-ranking on Long Samples:** The CLVP model, used for re-ranking, was only trained on clips up to 13 seconds long, which may limit its effectiveness on longer audio samples (2305.07243.pdf, p. 10, 12).
*   **Architecture Limitations:** The author notes that the diffusion decoder's architecture, which omits feed-forward blocks, was a "poor design decision" and should be improved in future work (2305.07243.pdf, p. 12).
*   **Sampling Rate Discrepancy:** The main model stack operates at 22kHz, but the diffusion decoder outputs MEL spectrograms computed from 24kHz audio to be compatible with the pre-trained UnivNet vocoder. This was not done for performance reasons and is a point for future improvement (2305.07243.pdf, p. 11).

### Recommendations:
*   **Use a GPU:** Inference on a CPU is extremely slow (on the order of hours for a sentence) and a GPU is strongly recommended (tortoise-tts.ipynb).
*   **Break Up Long Texts:** For texts longer than ~400 tokens, it is recommended to split them into smaller segments for inference (tortoise/api.py; tortoise/read.py).
*   **Use Presets:** For general use, the provided presets (`ultra_fast`, `fast`, `standard`, `high_quality`) offer good starting points for balancing quality and speed (tortoise/api.py).
*   **Future Improvements:** The paper's "Future Work" section suggests several architectural improvements for those looking to build upon this work, including using relative positional encodings, training CLVP on longer sequences, and adding feed-forward blocks to the diffusion decoder (2305.07243.pdf, p. 12).
