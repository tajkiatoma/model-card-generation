## Model Details
This section provides fundamental information about the model, helping stakeholders understand its context and key characteristics.

### Person or organization developing model:
The model was developed by Sanchit Gandhi, Patrick von Platen, and Alexander M. Rush from Hugging Face (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1).

### Model date:
The paper describing the model was submitted to arXiv on November 1, 2023 (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1).

### Model version:
The model is named Distil-Whisper. It is a distilled version of OpenAI's Whisper model. The paper presents two variants: `distil-medium.en` and `distil-large-v2`, which are distilled from the Whisper `medium.en` and `large-v2` models, respectively (2311.00430.pdf, p. 6, 8). These distilled models are significantly faster and smaller than their teacher models while maintaining comparable performance (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1). For example, `distil-large-v2` is 5.8 times faster and has 51% fewer parameters than `large-v2` (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1).

### Model type:
Distil-Whisper is a sequence-to-sequence (Seq2Seq) Transformer model for Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1, 3). It is created through knowledge distillation from a larger Whisper model (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1).

**Architecture:**
*   The model consists of a Transformer-based encoder and decoder (2311.00430.pdf, p. 3).
*   The encoder is entirely copied from the teacher model and is frozen during training (2311.00430.pdf, p. 6, Figure 1).
*   The decoder is significantly smaller, consisting of only two decoder layers, which are initialized from the first and last decoder layers of the teacher model (2311.00430.pdf, p. 6, Figure 1).

**Model Size:**
*   **distil-large-v2:** 32 encoder layers, 2 decoder layers, 1280 model width, 20 attention heads, and 756 million parameters (2311.00430.pdf, p. 7, Table 3).
*   **distil-medium.en:** 24 encoder layers, 2 decoder layers, 1024 model width, 16 attention heads, and 394 million parameters (2311.00430.pdf, p. 7, Table 3).

The model uses a `WhisperTokenizer` (tokenizer_config.json.txt) with a vocabulary size of 51,865, which includes special tokens for multilingual transcription and task specification (vocab.json.txt, added_tokens.json.txt). The model processes audio in 30-second segments (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5).

### Training details:
Distil-Whisper was trained using knowledge distillation, where a smaller "student" model learns to mimic a larger "teacher" model (Whisper) (2311.00430.pdf, p. 3).

*   **Algorithm:** The training objective is a weighted sum of two losses:
    1.  **Pseudo-Labelling (PL) Loss:** The student model is trained on pseudo-labels generated by the teacher model (Whisper large-v2) instead of the ground truth text. This is a form of sequence-level knowledge distillation (2311.00430.pdf, p. 4).
    2.  **Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence Loss:** The student model's full probability distribution over the vocabulary is trained to match the teacher's distribution at each token. This is a form of word-level knowledge distillation (2311.00430.pdf, p. 4).
*   **Training Objective:** The final objective is `L_KD = 0.8 * L_KL + 1.0 * L_PL` (2311.00430.pdf, p. 4).
*   **Data Filtering:** To ensure high-quality training data, a heuristic was used to filter the pseudo-labelled data. Samples where the Word Error Rate (WER) between the normalized ground-truth and the normalized pseudo-label exceeded a threshold of 10% were discarded (2311.00430.pdf, p. 4, 11).
*   **Initialization:** The student model's encoder is a direct copy of the teacher's encoder and is frozen during training. The 2-layer decoder is initialized by copying the weights from the first and last layers of the teacher's decoder (2311.00430.pdf, p. 4, 6).
*   **Hyperparameters:** The model was trained for 80,000 steps (8 epochs) with a batch size of 256 on TPU v4-8 accelerators. An AdamW optimizer was used with a slanted triangular learning rate schedule, peaking at 1e-4 (2311.00430.pdf, p. 23, Table 15).

## Intended Use
This section outlines the intended applications of the model.

### Primary intended uses:
The primary use of Distil-Whisper is for Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) for the English language (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1, 3). It is specifically designed for low-latency or resource-constrained environments where running larger models like Whisper is challenging (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1).

**Capabilities:**
*   **Short-form and Long-form Transcription:** The model can transcribe both short audio clips and long-form audio files (e.g., meetings, podcasts) using a chunking algorithm (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5, 8).
*   **Robustness:** It maintains robustness to different audio domains (e.g., government, audiobooks, telephone calls) and noisy acoustic conditions (2311.00430.pdf, p. 2).
*   **Speculative Decoding:** Distil-Whisper is designed to be used as a faster "assistant" model for the larger Whisper model in a speculative decoding setup. This can speed up the original Whisper model's inference by 2 times while mathematically ensuring the exact same output (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1, 5).

The model takes a 30-second audio input and outputs the transcribed text (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5).

### Primary intended users:
The primary users are developers and researchers in the field of speech recognition who need a fast and efficient ASR model for deployment in applications with limited computational resources or strict latency requirements (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1).

### Out-of-scope uses:
The models described in the paper (`distil-medium.en`, `distil-large-v2`) are intended for English ASR only (2311.00430.pdf, p. 3, Table 1). Using them for other languages is out-of-scope. The model is not designed to be a generative language model for tasks other than speech transcription. The paper also notes that the model is still prone to some repetition errors on long-form audio, though less so than the original Whisper model (2311.00430.pdf, p. 10).

## How to Use
This section outlines how to use the model. 

The paper states that inference code is publicly available at `https://github.com/huggingface/distil-whisper` (2311.00430.pdf, p. 2). The model can be used in several ways:

1.  **Standard Short-Form Transcription:** For audio clips up to 30 seconds, the model can be used directly for inference (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5).
2.  **Chunked Long-Form Transcription:** For audio longer than 30 seconds, a chunking algorithm is used. The audio is split into smaller, overlapping segments that can be processed in parallel through batching. The final transcription is generated by joining the text from each chunk at the overlapping strides (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5). This method is reported to be up to 9 times faster than sequential transcription (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5).
3.  **Speculative Decoding:** Distil-Whisper can act as an assistant model to a larger Whisper model. In this setup, Distil-Whisper generates a sequence of candidate tokens, which are then verified in a single forward pass by the main model. This accelerates the main model's inference by up to 2.4 times while guaranteeing identical output (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5, 11).

## Factors
This section addresses variables that may impact the model's performance.

### Relevant factors:
The model's performance is influenced by various factors related to acoustic conditions, speaker characteristics, and audio domains. The development and evaluation focused on ensuring robustness across these factors (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1, 7).
*   **Acoustic Conditions:** The model was evaluated on its robustness to additive noise, including white noise and "pub noise" (simulating a busy restaurant) (2311.00430.pdf, p. 7).
*   **Domains:** The training and evaluation data cover 10 distinct domains, such as interviews, podcasts, telephone conversations, narrated content, and meetings (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5, Table 2).
*   **Speakers:** The training data includes over 18,260 speakers with diverse accents and speaking styles (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5, Table 2).

### Evaluation factors:
The model was evaluated on factors of data distribution and acoustic noise (2311.00430.pdf, p. 7).
*   **Data Distribution:** Performance was measured on both in-distribution (ID) datasets (from the same sources as the training data) and out-of-distribution (OOD) datasets to test generalization in a zero-shot setting (2311.00430.pdf, p. 7).
*   **Noise:** The model's WER was measured on the LibriSpeech test-clean dataset with varying levels of additive white noise and pub noise, measured by Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) (2311.00430.pdf, p. 7, Figure 2).

## Metrics
This section describes how the model's performance is evaluated.

### Model performance measures:
*   **Word Error Rate (WER):** This is the primary metric used to measure transcription accuracy. Text is normalized using the Whisper English normaliser before WER calculation to penalize only transcription errors, not formatting differences (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1, 23).
*   **Long-Form Transcription Metrics:** To quantify hallucinations and other errors in long-form audio, the following metrics were used (2311.00430.pdf, p. 8):
    *   **5-gram Duplicates (5-Dup.):** The number of repeated 5-gram word duplicates, used to measure repetitive hallucinations.
    *   **Insertion Error Rate (IER):** Measures the frequency of hallucinated text not present in the audio.
    *   **Substitution Error Rate (SER):** Measures the frequency of substituted words.
    *   **Deletion Error Rate (DER):** Measures the frequency of deleted words.
*   **Latency:** Measured as a relative speed-up compared to the Whisper large-v2 model and as a Real-Time Factor (RTF), which is the ratio of inference time to audio duration (2311.00430.pdf, p. 8, 29).

### Decision thresholds:
A Word Error Rate (WER) threshold, denoted by `λ`, was used during the training data creation process. Pseudo-labels generated by the teacher model were filtered out if their normalized WER against the ground-truth text was greater than `λ`. A threshold of `λ = 10%` was found to provide a good trade-off between data quality and quantity (2311.00430.pdf, p. 4, 11, Table 10).

### Variation approaches:
Model performance is reported as a macro-average WER across multiple datasets to assess overall and effective robustness. This includes averages over 11 in-distribution (ID) datasets, 4 out-of-distribution (OOD) datasets, and all 15 datasets combined (2311.00430.pdf, p. 24, Table 16).

## Evaluation Data
This section provides details about the datasets used to evaluate the model.

### Datasets:
The model was evaluated on a total of 15 short-form and 4 long-form datasets (2311.00430.pdf, p. 7).

**Short-Form Datasets (2311.00430.pdf, p. 24, Table 16):**
*   **In-Distribution (ID):** Test splits of the 11 datasets used for training, including AMI IHM, AMI SDM, Call Home, Common Voice 13, GigaSpeech, LibriSpeech (clean and other), People's Speech, SwitchBoard, TED-LIUM, and Voxpopuli.
*   **Out-of-Distribution (OOD):** Four datasets not seen during training:
    *   **CHIME-4:** 7 hours of news broadcast audio (2311.00430.pdf, p. 7, Table 4).
    *   **Earnings-22:** 115 hours of financial meetings (2311.00430.pdf, p. 7, Table 4).
    *   **FLEURS:** 2 hours of narrated Wikipedia text (2311.00430.pdf, p. 7, Table 4).
    *   **SPGISpeech:** 100 hours of financial meetings (2311.00430.pdf, p. 7, Table 4).

**Long-Form Datasets (2311.00430.pdf, p. 8, Table 4):**
*   **Earnings-21:** 39 hours of company earnings calls.
*   **Earnings-22:** 115 hours of financial meetings.
*   **Meanwhile:** 1 hour of TV show segments.
*   **Rev 16:** 16 hours of podcast recordings.

### Motivation:
The diverse collection of datasets was chosen to rigorously evaluate the model's ability to generalize across different domains, speakers, and acoustic conditions. The inclusion of OOD datasets is crucial for measuring the model's zero-shot performance and robustness, which is a key feature of the original Whisper model that Distil-Whisper aims to retain (2311.00430.pdf, p. 7).

### Preprocessing:
*   **Normalization:** All transcriptions (both predicted and ground-truth) were standardized using the Whisper English normaliser before WER calculation (2311.00430.pdf, p. 23).
*   **Chunking:** For long-form evaluation, audio files were segmented into smaller chunks with overlaps to enable parallel processing (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5).
*   **Noise Addition:** For noise robustness tests, additive white noise or pub noise was applied to the LibriSpeech test-clean dataset at various Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNRs) (2311.00430.pdf, p. 7).

## Training Data
This section provides details about the datasets used to train the model.

### Datasets:
A large-scale corpus of 21,170 hours of English speech was assembled by combining nine publicly available datasets (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5, Table 2). The combined dataset contains over 18,260 speakers across 10 distinct domains (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5). The datasets are:
*   People's Speech (12,000h)
*   GigaSpeech (2,500h)
*   Common Voice 13 (2,400h)
*   Fisher (1,960h)
*   LibriSpeech (960h)
*   VoxPopuli (540h)
*   TED-LIUM (450h)
*   SwitchBoard (260h)
*   AMI (100h)

A link to the collection of training datasets is provided: `https://huggingface.co/collections/distil-whisper/training-datasets-6538d05c69721489d1db1e49` (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5, footnote 1).

### Motivation:
The datasets were chosen to create a large and diverse training corpus, inspired by the SpeechStew methodology (Chan et al., 2021). This diversity is intended to ensure the distilled model inherits the robustness of the teacher model to various acoustic conditions, domains, and speaker styles (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1, 5).

### Preprocessing:
The training process did not use the ground-truth transcriptions directly. Instead, it relied on pseudo-labelling (2311.00430.pdf, p. 4).
1.  **Pseudo-Label Generation:** The Whisper `large-v2` model was used to generate transcriptions (pseudo-labels) for the entire 21,170-hour training corpus using greedy decoding (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5).
2.  **Filtering:** A simple heuristic was applied to filter for high-quality pseudo-labels. For each sample, the Word Error Rate (WER) was calculated between the generated pseudo-label and the original ground-truth label (after both were normalized). If the WER was higher than a 10% threshold, the sample was discarded from the training set (2311.00430.pdf, p. 4, 11). This filtering removed approximately 45.4% of the initial data (2311.00430.pdf, p. 12, Table 10).

## Memory or Hardware Requirements
This section outlines the memory or hardware requirements for loading, deploying, and training the model.

### Loading Requirements:
The `distil-large-v2` model has 756M parameters, which is a 51% reduction compared to the 1.55B parameters of the Whisper `large-v2` model (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1, 7). This reduction in size makes it easier to load on resource-constrained hardware.

### Deploying Requirements:
Inference latency was benchmarked on a single A100 40GB GPU and a T4 16GB GPU using float16 precision (2311.00430.pdf, p. 23, 29).
*   The `distil-large-v2` model is 5.8 times faster than the `large-v2` model on short-form audio (batch size 1) (2311.00430.pdf, p. 8, Table 5).
*   When used as an assistant for speculative decoding, it increases the parameter count of the `large-v2` model by only 8% (2311.00430.pdf, p. 10).

### Training or Fine-tuning Requirements:
The model was trained using data parallelism across TPU v4-8 accelerators with bfloat16 precision and gradient checkpointing (2311.00430.pdf, p. 22).

## Caveats and Recommendations
This section lists unresolved issues and provides guidance for users.

### Caveats:
*   **Repetition Errors:** While Distil-Whisper is less prone to hallucinations than the original Whisper model, it is still more susceptible to repetition errors on long-form audio compared to encoder-only CTC-based models like wav2vec 2.0 (2311.00430.pdf, p. 10).
*   **Substitution Errors:** The distilled models show a higher substitution error rate (SER) compared to the teacher model, indicating they are subject to more word substitution errors (2311.00430.pdf, p. 10, Table 8).
*   **Encoder Distillation:** Jointly distilling the encoder and decoder to further reduce model size leads to significant performance degradation. The paper suggests that a deep encoder is "paramount for maintaining strong WER performance" (2311.00430.pdf, p. 12).
*   **Speculative Decoding at High Batch Sizes:** The latency benefits of speculative decoding diminish and can even become negative at higher batch sizes (e.g., batch size 16) (2311.00430.pdf, p. 28, Table 22).

### Recommendations:
*   **Hardware for Deployment:** For batch sizes greater than 1, higher memory GPUs like the A100 are preferential over consumer-grade GPUs like the T4, where performance stagnates (2311.00430.pdf, p. 30).
*   **Use of Flash Attention 2:** It is recommended to use Flash Attention 2 for inference, as it provides significant speed improvements, especially at higher batch sizes (2311.00430.pdf, p. 29).
*   **Long-Form Transcription:** The chunk length for the long-form transcription algorithm should be tuned based on the audio distribution. For Distil-Whisper, a chunk length of 15 seconds yielded the best results on the TED-LIUM validation set (2311.00430.pdf, p. 28, Table 23).
*   **Speculative Decoding:** Use speculative decoding with lower batch sizes to achieve significant latency improvements while ensuring identical outputs to the original Whisper model (2311.00430.pdf, p. 28).
