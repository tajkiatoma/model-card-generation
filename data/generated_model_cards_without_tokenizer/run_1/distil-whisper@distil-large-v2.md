## Model Details
This section provides fundamental information about the model, helping stakeholders understand its context and key characteristics.

### Person or organization developing model:
The model was developed by Sanchit Gandhi, Patrick von Platen, and Alexander M. Rush from Hugging Face (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1).

### Model date:
The associated research paper was submitted to arXiv on November 1, 2023 (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1).

### Model version:
The model is named Distil-Whisper. The repository corresponds to the `distil-large-v2` version, which is a distilled variant of the Whisper `large-v2` model (2311.00430.pdf, p. 6). The primary difference is a significant reduction in the number of decoder layers from 32 in the teacher model to 2 in the student model, while retaining the full 32-layer encoder (2311.00430.pdf, p. 6, Figure 1; p. 7, Table 3). This architectural change results in a model that is 5.8 times faster and has 51% fewer parameters than the original Whisper `large-v2` (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1). Another version, `distil-medium.en`, was also developed by distilling the Whisper `medium.en` model (2311.00430.pdf, p. 6).

### Model type:
Distil-Whisper is a sequence-to-sequence (Seq2Seq) Transformer model designed for Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) (2311.00430.pdf, p. 3). Its architecture is specified as `WhisperForConditionalGeneration` (config.json.txt).

Key architectural details for the `distil-large-v2` version include:
*   **Encoder:** 32 layers, copied from the Whisper `large-v2` model (2311.00430.pdf, p. 6).
*   **Decoder:** 2 layers, initialized from the first and last decoder layers of the Whisper `large-v2` model (2311.00430.pdf, p. 6).
*   **Model Dimension (`d_model`):** 1280 (config.json.txt).
*   **Attention Heads:** 20 for both encoder and decoder (config.json.txt).
*   **Feed-Forward Network Dimension:** 5120 for both encoder and decoder (config.json.txt).
*   **Total Parameters:** 756 million (2311.00430.pdf, p. 7, Table 3).
*   **Vocabulary Size:** 51,865 (config.json.txt).
*   **Supported Context Length:** The model processes audio in fixed 30-second chunks (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5). The maximum number of generated tokens is 448 (config.json.txt).

The model takes 80-dimensional log-Mel spectrogram features from audio sampled at 16kHz as input and generates text tokens as output (config.json.txt; preprocessor_config.json.txt).

### Training details:
Distil-Whisper was trained using knowledge distillation (KD), a technique where a smaller "student" model learns to replicate the behavior of a larger "teacher" model (2311.00430.pdf, p. 3).

*   **Teacher Model:** Whisper `large-v2` (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5).
*   **Student Initialization:** The student model's 32-layer encoder was copied entirely from the teacher model and frozen during training. The student's 2-layer decoder was initialized by copying the weights from the first and last decoder layers of the teacher model (2311.00430.pdf, p. 6).
*   **Training Objective:** The model was trained on a weighted sum of two objectives:
    1.  **Pseudo-Labelling (PL):** A sequence-level KD loss where the student model is trained to predict transcriptions generated by the teacher model (pseudo-labels) (2311.00430.pdf, p. 4).
    2.  **Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence:** A word-level KD loss where the student model's full probability distribution over the vocabulary is trained to match the teacher's distribution at each step (2311.00430.pdf, p. 4).
*   **Pseudo-Label Filtering:** To ensure the quality of the training signal, a heuristic was applied where training samples were filtered out if the Word Error Rate (WER) between the teacher's pseudo-label and the ground-truth label exceeded a threshold of 10% (2311.00430.pdf, p. 4, 11).
*   **Hyperparameters:** The model was trained for 80,000 optimization steps with a batch size of 256. A slanted triangular learning rate schedule was used with a maximum learning rate of 1e-4. Training was performed on TPU v4-8 accelerators with bfloat16 precision (2311.00430.pdf, p. 6, 23, Table 15).

## Intended Use
This section outlines the intended applications of the model.

### Primary intended uses:
The primary intended use of Distil-Whisper is for robust Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) in English (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1). It is specifically designed as a smaller, faster alternative to the original Whisper model, making it suitable for deployment in low-latency or resource-constrained environments (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1).

The model's capabilities include:
*   **Speech Transcription:** Transcribing spoken English audio into text. The model can be tasked with `transcribe` (generation_config.json.txt).
*   **Speech Translation:** The model configuration includes a task ID for `translate`, indicating it can translate audio from a source language to English (generation_config.json.txt).
*   **Speculative Decoding Assistant:** Distil-Whisper is designed to be paired with the larger Whisper model for speculative decoding. In this setup, it acts as a faster "assistant" model that generates draft transcriptions, which are then verified by the main model. This can yield a 2x speed-up in inference while mathematically ensuring the output is identical to that of the original Whisper model (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1, 11).

The model takes audio as input and outputs a sequence of text tokens representing the transcription.

### Primary intended users:
The primary intended users are developers, researchers, and businesses who require a high-performance ASR model that is more computationally efficient than the original Whisper `large-v2` model. This includes those building applications with real-time transcription needs or deploying models on hardware with limited resources (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1).

### Out-of-scope uses:
*   **Non-English Languages:** While the underlying Whisper architecture is multilingual, the distillation process and all evaluations reported in the paper are for English-only models (`distil-medium.en` and `distil-large-v2`). Therefore, its performance on other languages is not validated and should be considered out-of-scope (2311.00430.pdf, p. 6-8).
*   **Critical Safety Applications:** The model can produce transcription errors, including hallucinations (generating text not present in the audio), although it is less prone to them than the original Whisper model (2311.00430.pdf, p. 8). It should not be used in safety-critical applications where transcription errors could lead to harm without human oversight.
*   **Speaker Identification/Verification:** The model is designed for speech-to-text conversion and is not intended for identifying or verifying speakers.

## How to Use
This section outlines how to use the model.

The model is an encoder-decoder Transformer that processes audio for speech recognition. The general workflow is as follows:

1.  **Load the Model and Processor:** Load the `WhisperForConditionalGeneration` model and the corresponding `WhisperProcessor` (config.json.txt; preprocessor_config.json.txt). The processor handles converting raw audio into the format required by the model.
2.  **Preprocess Audio:** The input audio must be sampled at 16,000 Hz (preprocessor_config.json.txt). The processor will then convert the audio into a log-Mel spectrogram (config.json.txt).
3.  **Generate Transcription:** Pass the processed audio features to the model's `generate` method to produce the output token IDs.
4.  **Decode Output:** Use the processor to decode the token IDs into human-readable text.

The model supports several tasks, which can be specified during generation:
*   `transcribe`: Performs in-language speech-to-text (generation_config.json.txt).
*   `translate`: Translates the speech into English (generation_config.json.txt).

The model is multilingual, supporting 99 languages as indicated by the `lang_to_id` mapping in its configuration. The default language is English (`<|en|>`) (generation_config.json.txt).

For transcribing audio files longer than 30 seconds, a "chunked long-form transcription" algorithm is recommended. This involves splitting the audio into overlapping chunks, transcribing each chunk independently (potentially in parallel), and stitching the results together by finding the longest common sequence between overlaps (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5).

### Relevant factors:
The model's performance is influenced by several factors, which were considered during its development and evaluation:
*   **Audio Domain:** The model was trained and evaluated on data from diverse domains, including audiobooks, telephone conversations, meetings, podcasts, parliamentary speeches, and financial earnings calls, to ensure robustness (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5, Table 2; p. 7, Table 4).

### Evaluation factors:
The model evaluation specifically analyzed performance across the following factors:
*   **In-Distribution (ID) vs. Out-of-Distribution (OOD) Data:** The model was evaluated on both datasets seen during training (ID) and unseen datasets (OOD) to measure its ability to generalize (2311.00430.pdf, p. 7).
*   **Noise Levels:** The model's performance was systematically evaluated on the LibriSpeech dataset with varying levels of additive white noise and "pub noise" to measure its robustness to acoustic degradation (2311.00430.pdf, p. 7, 9, Figure 2).

## Metrics
This section describes how the model's performance is evaluated.

### Model performance measures:
The primary metric used to assess transcription accuracy is the **Word Error Rate (WER)**, which measures the number of substitutions, deletions, and insertions required to match the predicted text to the reference text (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1).

For a more detailed analysis of errors, especially in long-form transcription, the following metrics were also used:
*   **Insertion Error Rate (IER):** Measures the rate of words incorrectly inserted by the model.
*   **Substitution Error Rate (SER):** Measures the rate of words incorrectly substituted by the model.
*   **Deletion Error Rate (DER):** Measures the rate of words incorrectly omitted by the model.
*   **Repeated 5-gram word duplicates (5-Dup.):** Quantifies the number of repetitive sequences, which is a common failure mode (hallucination) in long-form transcription (2311.00430.pdf, p. 8, Table 8).

### Decision thresholds:
During the creation of the training data, a decision threshold was used to filter the pseudo-labels generated by the teacher model. Any training sample where the WER between the teacher's prediction and the ground truth was greater than **10%** was discarded. This threshold was found to provide a good trade-off between retaining high-quality training data and having a sufficient quantity of data (2311.00430.pdf, p. 11, Table 10).

### Variation approaches:
Model performance is reported as a macro-average WER across multiple datasets to provide a robust measure of overall and out-of-distribution performance (2311.00430.pdf, p. 8, Table 5; p. 24, Table 16). For noise robustness, performance is plotted across a range of Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNRs) to show how performance degrades as noise increases (2311.00430.pdf, p. 10, Figure 2).

## Evaluation Data
This section provides details about the datasets used to evaluate the model.

### Datasets:
The model was evaluated on a total of 15 short-form and 4 long-form English speech recognition datasets.

**Short-Form Datasets (2311.00430.pdf, p. 7, 24):**
*   **In-Distribution (ID):** Test splits from the 11 datasets corresponding to the training corpora, including AMI, Call Home, Common Voice 13, GigaSpeech, LibriSpeech (clean and other), People's Speech, SwitchBoard, TED-LIUM, and VoxPopuli.
*   **Out-of-Distribution (OOD):** Four datasets not used in training:
    *   **CHIME-4:** 7 hours of news broadcast recordings in noisy environments.
    *   **Earnings-22:** 115 hours of financial earnings calls with a diverse range of speakers and accents.
    *   **FLEURS:** 2 hours of narrated Wikipedia text.
    *   **SPGISpeech:** 100 hours of financial meetings.

**Long-Form Datasets (OOD) (2311.00430.pdf, p. 7, Table 4):**
*   **Earnings-21:** 39 hours of company earnings calls.
*   **Earnings-22:** 115 hours of company earnings calls.
*   **Meanwhile:** A collection of 64 segments from "The Late Show with Stephen Colbert".
*   **Rev 16:** 16 podcast recordings used for benchmarking production ASR systems.

### Motivation:
This broad collection of datasets was chosen to thoroughly assess the model's primary objective: retaining the robustness of the original Whisper model across diverse acoustic conditions, domains, speaker styles, and accents. The use of OOD datasets is crucial for measuring the model's ability to generalize in a zero-shot setting, which is a key feature of the Whisper family of models (2311.00430.pdf, p. 7).

### Preprocessing:
Before calculating WER, all transcribed text (both model predictions and ground truth labels) is standardized using the **Whisper English normaliser**. This process involves removing or converting specific words, symbols, and numeric expressions, as well as managing whitespace and spelling variations (e.g., converting British to American English spellings, as detailed in `normalizer.json.txt`). This ensures that the evaluation penalizes actual transcription errors rather than formatting differences (2311.00430.pdf, p. 23). For long-form evaluation, audio files are segmented into shorter chunks using a specialized algorithm (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5).

## Training Data
This section provides details about the datasets used to train the model.

### Datasets:
The model was trained on a large-scale corpus assembled from nine publicly available English speech recognition datasets, totaling **21,170 hours** of audio. This combined dataset encompasses over **18,260 speakers** and 10 distinct domains (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5).

The constituent datasets are (2311.00430.pdf, p. 6, Table 2):
*   **People's Speech:** 12,000 hours (Government, interviews)
*   **GigaSpeech:** 2,500 hours (Audiobook, podcast, YouTube)
*   **Common Voice 13:** 2,400 hours (Narrated Wikipedia)
*   **Fisher:** 1,960 hours (Telephone conversations)
*   **LibriSpeech:** 960 hours (Audiobooks)
*   **VoxPopuli:** 540 hours (European Parliament)
*   **TED-LIUM:** 450 hours (TED talks)
*   **SwitchBoard:** 260 hours (Telephone conversations)
*   **AMI:** 100 hours (Meetings)

### Motivation:
The choice of a large and diverse training set was inspired by the training methodology of the original Whisper model. By combining data from many different domains and speakers, the goal was to ensure that the distilled model would maintain the high level of robustness and generalization that characterizes the teacher model (2311.00430.pdf, p. 1, 6).

### Preprocessing:
The training process relied on pseudo-labelling, where the Whisper `large-v2` model first transcribed the entire 21,170-hour training set. The preprocessing steps were as follows:
1.  **Audio Processing:** Raw audio was sampled at 16kHz and converted into 80-channel log-Mel spectrograms, with each frame computed over a 25ms window with a 10ms stride (preprocessor_config.json.txt; config.json.txt).
2.  **Pseudo-Label Generation:** The teacher model generated transcriptions (pseudo-labels) for all audio samples using greedy decoding (2311.00430.pdf, p. 5).
3.  **Text Normalization:** Both the ground-truth transcriptions and the generated pseudo-labels were normalized using the Whisper English normaliser (2311.00430.pdf, p. 4).
4.  **WER-based Filtering:** A WER was computed between the normalized ground-truth and the normalized pseudo-label for each sample. Any sample with a WER greater than 10% was discarded from the training set to filter out low-quality transcriptions (2311.00430.pdf, p. 4). This filtering removed 45.4% of the initial pseudo-labelled data (2311.00430.pdf, p. 12, Table 10).

## Quantitative Analyses
This section presents disaggregated evaluation results.

### Unitary results:
The model's performance was analyzed across several individual factors.

**Performance on OOD Datasets (`distil-large-v2`):**
*   **Average OOD Short-Form WER:** 10.1% (2311.00430.pdf, p. 8, Table 5).
*   **Average OOD Long-Form WER:** 11.6% (2311.00430.pdf, p. 8, Table 5).
*   **Per-Dataset Short-Form WER:** CHIME-4 (14.0%), Earnings-22 (16.9%), FLEURS (6.3%), SPGISpeech (3.3%) (2311.00430.pdf, p. 24, Table 16).
*   **Per-Dataset Long-Form WER:** Earnings 21 (11.2%), Earnings 22 (15.1%), Meanwhile (7.8%), Rev 16 (12.2%) (2311.00430.pdf, p. 24, Table 17).

**Robustness to Additive Noise:**
*   Performance degrades less severely under noise compared to models trained only on LibriSpeech. As noise becomes more intensive (e.g., below 10 dB SNR), its performance approaches that of the original Whisper model, demonstrating similar noise robustness due to sharing the same frozen encoder (2311.00430.pdf, p. 9, Figure 2).

**Robustness to Hallucinations (Long-Form):**
*   Compared to Whisper `large-v2`, `distil-large-v2` has 1.3 times fewer repetition errors (18503 vs 23792 5-Dup.) and a lower insertion error rate (2.1% vs 3.3% IER), indicating it is less prone to hallucinations (2311.00430.pdf, p. 10, 11, Table 8).
*   However, it has a higher substitution error rate (5.3% vs 3.9% SER) (2311.00430.pdf, p. 11, Table 8).

### Deploying Requirements:
Inference performance was benchmarked on two types of GPUs. These represent typical hardware for deployment:
*   **NVIDIA A100 (40GB):** On this GPU, using Flash Attention 2 and a batch size of 1, the `distil-large-v2` model achieves a real-time factor (RTF) of 24.2 x 10⁻³, meaning it can transcribe audio ~41 times faster than real-time (2311.00430.pdf, p. 30, Table 24).
*   **NVIDIA T4 (16GB):** On this consumer-grade GPU, using Flash Attention and a batch size of 1, the model achieves an RTF of 27.4 x 10⁻³, transcribing audio ~36 times faster than real-time (2311.00430.pdf, p. 30, Table 25).

Performance varies with batch size, and an A100 GPU is recommended for batch sizes greater than 1 to avoid performance stagnation (2311.00430.pdf, p. 30).

### Training or Fine-tuning Requirements:
The model was trained on **TPU v4-8 accelerators** using **bfloat16** mixed-precision and gradient checkpointing to manage memory usage (2311.00430.pdf, p. 22). This indicates that training or full fine-tuning requires significant computational resources, likely a multi-accelerator setup with high memory capacity.

## Caveats and Recommendations
This section lists unresolved issues and provides guidance for users.

### Caveats:
*   **Performance Trade-off:** While significantly faster, `distil-large-v2` has a slightly higher WER (10.1%) on average for short-form OOD tasks compared to the teacher model, Whisper `large-v2` (9.1%) (2311.00430.pdf, p. 8, Table 5).
*   **Higher Substitution Errors:** The distillation process resulted in a model that is more prone to substitution errors (SER of 5.3%) than the original model (SER of 3.9%), even though it makes fewer insertion errors (2311.00430.pdf, p. 11, Table 8).
*   **Importance of Deep Encoder:** Attempts to distill the encoder by reducing its depth led to significant performance degradation. This suggests that a deep encoder is paramount for maintaining the model's strong ASR performance, limiting the potential for further parameter reduction without performance loss (2311.00430.pdf, p. 12, Table 12).
*   **Repetition Errors:** Although improved, the model is still susceptible to repetition errors in long-form transcription, a challenge that remains for Seq2Seq ASR models in general (2311.00430.pdf, p. 10).

### Recommendations:
*   **Use for Efficient ASR:** The model is recommended as a drop-in replacement for Whisper `large-v2` in scenarios where inference speed and computational cost are more critical than a minor decrease in accuracy.
*   **Speculative Decoding:** For applications that require the exact output of Whisper `large-v2` but need faster inference, it is highly recommended to use Distil-Whisper as an assistant model in a speculative decoding pipeline. This achieves a 2x speed-up with no change in output (2311.00430.pdf, p. 11).
*   **Long-Form Transcription Settings:** When transcribing long audio files, use the chunked transcription algorithm. The optimal chunk length for `distil-large-v2` is around 15 seconds, which differs from the 30-second optimum for the original Whisper model (2311.00430.pdf, p. 29, Table 23).
*   **Use Flash Attention 2:** For deployment, enabling Flash Attention 2 is recommended as it significantly improves inference speed, especially at higher batch sizes, with a greater relative improvement for Distil-Whisper than for Whisper (2311.00430.pdf, p. 29).
